Nucor Raises Steel Prices Again: Inside the Fortress America Pricing Power Story at $1,040 a Ton

Nucor just pushed its hot rolled coil price to $1,040 a ton, $1,090 in California, the ninth consecutive price hike in a single run. At the same time, Steel Dynamics guided first quarter earnings to nearly double year over year, and domestic steelmakers are sitting on a 35% order backlog increase.

When a commodity producer raises prices nine times in a row and the order book keeps growing, that's not a cycle. That's a regime change, and it just got written into federal regulation as of April 6.

What actually changed on April 6

The Section 232 tariff structure on imported steel changed from a 50% tariff on the embedded steel content of a finished product to a 50% tariff on the full customs value of any product containing steel. On paper that sounds like a technical tweak. In practice it's a sledgehammer.

Under the old rule, if you imported a $1,000 piece of equipment with $200 of steel in it, you paid 50% on the $200, so $100 in tariff. Under the new rule, you pay 50% on the full $1,000, so $500 in tariff. Every finished good that contains steel just got a lot more expensive to import. That doesn't just protect Nucor and Steel Dynamics. It widens their moat by a full order of magnitude.

Cleveland-Cliffs (CLF) is the other name that benefits, because it sells the majority of its steel into the domestic auto supply chain, and the new tariff structure makes foreign auto parts and vehicles that use foreign steel much less competitive.

Nucor's historic breakeven is around $500 a ton. At $1,040 the company is earning more than two times breakeven on every ton shipped. That's not a small overshoot. That's the kind of pricing power you see in branded pharmaceuticals, not in flat rolled steel.

Why this is different from every other steel cycle

Steel cycles have boomed and busted on the same pattern for 50 years. Prices spike, domestic producers over build capacity, imports flood in once prices are high, and the whole thing crashes. That's the movie you've seen before. This time three things are different.

First, imports can't flood in because the new Section 232 structure taxes them on full customs value. Second, domestic capacity isn't expanding fast. US steel capacity utilization is running below 80%, which tells you producers are choosing pricing over volume. Third, the demand side isn't just infrastructure. It's reshoring, data center construction, grid upgrades, and defense. All four of those are multi year pipelines, not single quarter orders.

The 35% backlog increase is the number that matters most. Backlog is forward revenue you've already locked in. A 35% backlog expansion across the domestic industry means the next two quarters are already priced, and the price is high.

Is the run priced in

This is the hard question. NUE is up meaningfully from a year ago, and the forward PE on current estimates is no longer cheap. So the question isn't whether the stock is historically cheap. It's whether the earnings estimates have caught up to the pricing reality.

Here's the math. At $1,040 HRC and 52% gross margins, Nucor's run rate earnings power is higher than the street consensus by roughly 15 to 20%. Analysts tend to lag commodity price changes by a quarter or two because they don't want to assume current prices hold. If you believe the tariff regime holds and the backlog holds, earnings estimates have to come up again, and the stock still has room.

If you think the pricing breaks on the next auto recession, or that the tariffs get unwound by a future administration, then the stock is fully priced and the risk reward is less favorable. That's the bull bear split in one sentence.

My take: the tariff structure is politically sticky. No one in Washington wants to be the person who "killed domestic steel" in the next election cycle. The backlog is real. The pricing is real. The thesis is intact, and the question for investors is whether to own the leader or the cheaper name, not whether to own any of them.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why did Nucor raise prices nine times in a row?

Because demand kept absorbing each hike without killing volume. When you can raise price without losing customers, you raise again. That's the definition of pricing power, and it's reinforced by the Section 232 tariff structure that keeps imports out.

What's the difference between the old and new Section 232 tariffs?

The old rule taxed 50% on the embedded steel content of imported finished goods. The new rule, effective April 6, taxes 50% on the full customs value of any product containing steel. That's a roughly 5x increase in effective tariff burden for most manufactured imports.

Is Nucor a better buy than Steel Dynamics right now?

Nucor is the higher quality name with more consistent execution. Steel Dynamics trades at a slight forward multiple discount and has more operating torque. For a core position, most investors prefer NUE. For more upside with more risk, STLD makes sense.

What happens to the thesis if oil crashes and a recession hits?

Steel demand softens, but the tariff wall stays up. Nucor's breakeven is $500 a ton versus the current $1,040, so even a 30% price drop still leaves the company very profitable. The cyclical downside is real but limited by the structural floor.

Could the Section 232 rule get rolled back?

Unlikely in the near term. Steel tariffs are one of the most bipartisan policies in Washington. Both parties have used them. A rollback would be politically expensive and is not on the current administration's agenda.
