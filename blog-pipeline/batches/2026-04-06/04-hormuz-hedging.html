How to Hedge a Hormuz Close: Gold at $4,660, Bitcoin at $69K, and What Actually Works

Gold is trading between $4,656 and $4,670 an ounce Monday morning, Brent crude just printed $109.77, and there's an 8pm deadline on a live shooting war that could shut the Strait of Hormuz before you finish your coffee tomorrow. If that strait closes, oil hits $150 to $200 within hours, not days.

So the question isn't whether you should hedge. It's which hedges actually work when the worst case shows up on a Tuesday morning, and which ones will leave you holding a bag of expensive nothing.

The setup in one paragraph

Central banks are buying gold at roughly 585 tonnes per quarter, the highest sustained pace on record. Silver is up 28% year to date. Bitcoin popped 3% to $69,355 on a ceasefire headline that markets are treating as a short squeeze, not a resolution. The VIX is sitting between 20 and 23, which is elevated but not panicked. The 10 year Treasury yield is at 4.339%, the Fed funds rate is at 3.50 to 3.75%, and WTI is at $112.41. Every one of these numbers tells you the same thing: the market is pricing risk, but it's not pricing disaster.

Your job, if you're worried about Hormuz, is to buy disaster insurance before the disaster shows up. That means you have to know what actually protects you and what just feels protective.

What works, ranked

There are only four instruments that reliably perform during an energy shock combined with a geopolitical crisis. Physical gold and gold ETFs. Short duration Treasuries. Energy equities with domestic production. And, if you can stomach the roll cost, long oil futures or oil ETFs like USO.

Gold works because central banks are already buying it, and a supply shock just accelerates what's already happening. Short Treasuries work because in a crisis the Fed cuts, and short bills give you the cash to buy the dip later. Energy equities work because if oil goes to $180, XLE holdings print money, and they don't carry the roll cost of futures.

What doesn't work, and why people keep believing it does

Bitcoin is not a crisis hedge. It's a liquidity asset. When the VIX spikes and margin calls hit, Bitcoin sells off with equities because the same people who own Nasdaq call options also own Bitcoin, and they sell whatever has a bid. The 3% pop to $69,355 today is a short squeeze on a ceasefire headline, not a flight to safety. If Hormuz closes tomorrow, Bitcoin does not go up. It gets sold to meet margin calls on everything else.

Long duration bonds also don't work here. The standard "stocks down, bonds up" trade breaks when inflation is the problem. If oil hits $180, the 10 year yield doesn't fall, it rises, because the market has to price higher inflation for longer. That's why you want SHY and BIL, not TLT. Short duration gives you the rate cut optionality without the inflation risk.

Defensive tech is the third trap. In 2022 you learned that Microsoft and Google sell off just as hard as the Nasdaq when rates and geopolitics hit at the same time. Mega cap tech is not a safe haven. It's a momentum trade wearing a safety costume.

Two things jump out. First, gold is the only asset that is positive in every single crisis on that list. Second, long bonds failed in 2022 precisely because the crisis was also inflationary. A Hormuz close is an inflationary crisis. It rhymes with 1973 and 2022, not with 2008 or 2020.

That means your hedging template should be weighted toward 1973 playbook, not 2008 playbook. More gold, more energy, more short bills. Less long duration, less defensive tech, less Bitcoin.

The practical playbook for Monday afternoon

If you haven't hedged yet and you're worried about the 8pm deadline, here's the simplest move that actually protects you without overcommitting. Take 10% of your portfolio and split it. Half into IAU or physical gold. A quarter into XLE. A quarter into SHY or BIL. That gives you gold exposure for the tail risk, energy exposure for the supply shock, and short bills for the rate cut if the Fed reacts.

If you already own gold and energy, you don't need to chase. Gold at $4,660 is 17% off its $5,600 February peak, so you're not buying at the highs. If you're at 5% gold already, going to 8 or 10% is a prudent trim from equities, not a panic move.

The one thing you shouldn't do is buy Bitcoin at $69,355 thinking it's a hedge. That's not what it is. It's a liquidity bet on risk appetite, which is the exact opposite of what you want if Hormuz closes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is gold at $4,660 still a buy, or did I miss the move?

Gold is 17% below its February peak of $5,600, and central banks are still buying at record pace. If you're underweight, you haven't missed anything. If you're already at 10% allocation, you don't need to add.

What's the difference between GLD and IAU?

Both track gold bullion. IAU charges 0.25% versus GLD's 0.40%, so for a long term holding IAU is cheaper. GLD has more trading volume, which matters only if you're trading options on it.

Should I buy oil futures instead of XLE?

Only if you understand contango and roll cost. Retail investors almost always get chewed up by futures because the front month rolls at a loss in contango markets. XLE gives you oil upside through equities without that drag.

What if Hormuz doesn't close and oil falls back to $90?

Then your gold allocation stays where it is, your energy trade loses 15 to 20%, and your short bills earn yield. That's a manageable drawdown on a hedge. The point of insurance is you hope you don't need it.

How much of my portfolio should be in hedges total?

For most investors, 10 to 20% across gold, energy, and short duration bills is enough. More than that and you're running an active geopolitical bet, not a hedge.
