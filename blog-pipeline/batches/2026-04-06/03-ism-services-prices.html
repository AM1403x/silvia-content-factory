ISM Services Prices at 70.7 Percent: A 42-Month High Just Killed the Fed Pivot

The ISM Services Prices Paid index hit 70.7 percent in March, up from 63 percent in February, marking the highest reading since October 2022 and a 42-month high. At the same time, the Employment sub-index slipped to 45.2 percent, the first contraction in four months.

That combination has a name and it's not recovery. It's stagflation-lite, and it just took the Fed's April pivot off the table before the meeting even started.

The Report Inside the Report

Headline ISM Services PMI printed at 54.0 percent in March, down 2.1 points from February's 56.1 percent. That's still expansion territory and the 21st straight month above 50. The headline is calm. The interior is not.

Business Activity cratered to 53.9 percent from 59.9 percent, the lowest reading since September 2025. New Orders actually jumped to 60.6 percent from 58.6 percent, hitting the highest level since February 2023. And the Prices Paid spike to 70.7 percent speaks for itself.

So read the interior back to yourself. Services firms are seeing strong order books, but activity is slowing, employment is contracting, and input costs are ripping. That's the sequence you get when a supply shock hits a late-cycle economy. Not a recovery, and not a clean slowdown either.

Look at that employment line. Services employment hasn't contracted since November 2025. Now it's back below 50 and dropping 6.6 points in a single month. That's not a normal cooling pattern. That's firms pulling back on headcount specifically because input costs are eating margins faster than they can raise output prices.

Why This Is the Worst Possible Combination for the Fed

The Fed's playbook is simple. Cut into cooling data. Hold into hot data. What happens when data cools and heats at the same time?

You get hawkish paralysis. The Fed can't cut, because Prices Paid at 70.7 percent pipes directly into core PCE within 60 days. Cutting here would be an inflation-policy own goal. But the Fed can't hike either, because services employment just flipped to contraction and New Orders at 60.6 percent mean the slowdown isn't driven by demand destruction, it's driven by margin compression. Hiking into that makes the contraction worse without touching the price problem.

That's how you get a hold with hawkish language on April 29. Fed funds futures already show 94.8 percent probability of a hold. The language is where the action is, and this ISM print guarantees that language gets meaner.

The pattern is ugly in both directions. Every time Prices Paid has printed above 70 percent in the last 15 years, the Fed has either been mid-tightening or pivoting into tightening. The only two instances where it resolved without rate action were 2011 and 2008, and both required external commodity reversals, not Fed choice.

In 2026, crude at $112 and the Iran ceasefire uncertainty mean that external commodity reversal is not on the table. The Fed has no exit.

Why Order Books Are Strong While Employment Contracts

New Orders at 60.6 percent looks like a contradiction next to Employment at 45.2 percent. It's not. It's a signal that services firms are getting business booked at higher prices but can't staff to fulfill it without compressing margins further.

That's the squeeze. Wages are still sticky at 3.5 percent year over year from the March payrolls report. Input costs are ripping. Firms are bidding for work they may not profitably deliver. You see this pattern in the last stage of an expansion before the order book eventually matches up with the reality of thinner margins, and then hiring stays weak while prices roll over.

The April 29 Meeting and What Comes After

Fed blackout begins April 18, which means the Fed gets 12 days of data, including March CPI and PPI, before officials go quiet. If CPI prints above 3.1 percent year over year, the hold language on April 29 turns outright hawkish, and the market will need to reprice the 2026 cut path.

Current futures are pricing just one cut through year-end 2026. If hawkish language lands, that drops to zero cuts, and the 2-year yield backs up 20 basis points. If any hint of a cut remains, equities get another reason to melt up even as the real economy contracts around the edges.

You should be positioned for a hold, not a cut. And you should be positioned for the S&P to discover this eventually, even if the reopening rally holds for another week. The disconnect between equity pricing and service-sector reality is the most important tension in the market right now.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is the ISM Services Prices Paid index so important?

It's a leading indicator for core PCE inflation with roughly a 60-day lag, and the Fed watches it because services inflation is the stickiest component of the CPI basket. A reading of 70.7 percent means a meaningful share of services firms are reporting higher input costs, which tends to flow through to final prices within one to two quarters.

What does a reading of 45.2 percent on Employment mean?

Any ISM sub-index below 50 signals contraction. At 45.2 percent, services employment is contracting at a meaningful pace for the first time in four months. That's a signal that firms are reducing headcount or not replacing attrition, typically because margins are under pressure.

Can the Fed cut rates if inflation stays high?

Historically no. The Fed has never cut rates with services Prices Paid above 70 percent unless an external commodity shock reversed first. With crude at $112 and Iran tensions unresolved, that reversal path is blocked. Cuts into rising prices would undermine the 2 percent target the Fed has been defending for three years.

What should you watch before the April 29 Fed meeting?

Three things. The March CPI print, specifically core services ex-shelter. The Strait of Hormuz status and WTI crude tape. And the Fed blackout period starting April 18, because any last pre-blackout speech is the clearest signal of the April 29 language you'll get.
