Section 232 Day One: What the New Steel and Aluminum Tariff Structure Means for Your Portfolio

At 12:01 am EDT on April 6, 2026, the US flipped its Section 232 tariff structure from 50 percent on embedded metal content to 25 percent on the full customs value of imported steel and aluminum goods, with Annex I-A articles taxed at 50 percent and Annex I-B at 25 percent. Products with less than 15 percent metal content by weight are now fully exempt.

This isn't a tweak. It's a rewrite of who pays, who collects, and which American factories just got a moat. If you own domestic mill equity, you already know. If you own a manufacturer that imports finished metal goods, you should be nervous.

What Actually Changed Overnight

The old Section 232 structure taxed only the metal content inside an imported product, which let foreign suppliers game the math by loading in cheap steel and claiming non-metal value. The new rule slaps 25 percent on the full customs value of the article, which means a $100 imported bracket with $40 of steel now gets hit on the full $100, not the $40.

That's a 2.5x effective increase for high-steel-content products and a full exemption for products where metal is less than 15 percent of weight. The bright line is the 15 percent threshold. Anything above it, you're paying on the full invoice. Anything below, you're out of the tariff net entirely.

The 10 percent domestic-melt tier is the giveaway. It's a direct subsidy dressed as a tariff, because anyone using US-melted steel pays a fraction of what foreign-melted competitors pay on the same finished product. Congress calls it Section 232. I'd call it industrial policy with a trade-law sticker on it.

Who Wins: Follow the Price Sheets

Nucor moved first. The company raised hot-rolled coil to $1,040 per ton nationally and $1,090 in California. That's a real-dollar increase, not a headline. Steel Dynamics guided Q1 earnings nearly double year over year on a 35 percent order backlog increase. Those aren't projections. They're the market already clearing higher because imports just got taxed out of the bidding.

Cleveland-Cliffs, the third leg of the domestic triad, hasn't announced guidance yet but sits in the same position with integrated iron-ore-to-steel operations. Any flat-rolled buyer who was comparing foreign versus domestic offers on April 5 has a very different spreadsheet on April 6.

The Express Fasteners lawsuit is worth watching. A mid-size Illinois importer is challenging the CBP assessment method on finished fasteners, arguing the 15 percent threshold is being applied without clear customs guidance. If they win, it blows a hole in the enforcement mechanism. If they lose, every finished-goods importer just saw their appeals ceiling capped.

The Drawback Loophole Nobody's Talking About

Drawback eligibility for UK, EU, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, and Canada trade partners means you can import, manufacture, and re-export the finished product and claim a refund on the 232 tariff. That's a real escape hatch for any company with export volume into those markets. It's also a paperwork nightmare that'll push small importers toward domestic sourcing even if the math barely works.

The practical effect is that large multinationals with in-house trade compliance teams keep importing under drawback. Small and mid-size domestic manufacturers without that infrastructure get squeezed into buying from Nucor or Steel Dynamics regardless of price. The big get bigger. The small get domestic.

What Happens to Steel Prices From Here

Nucor's $1,040 HRC level is a floor, not a peak. Historical precedent from the 2018 Section 232 round suggests domestic mills hold price discipline for two to three quarters before foreign supply finds workarounds. This time the drawback rules are narrower and the 15 percent content threshold is cleaner, so the workaround window is probably longer.

If you own the mills, you own pricing power into Q3 at least. If you own the downstream manufacturers, you're watching margin compression unless you've already hedged supply contracts into 2026. Earnings season starts in two weeks, and the first calls from industrial manufacturers are going to tell you who planned for this and who didn't.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is the new Section 232 tariff calculated?

The tariff is applied to the full customs value of the imported article, not just the embedded metal content. Annex I-A articles pay 50 percent on full value, Annex I-B articles pay 25 percent, and products with less than 15 percent metal by weight are exempt. The 10 percent tier applies to goods made from US-melted steel or US-smelted aluminum and copper.

Which companies benefit most from the new structure?

Domestic integrated mills benefit first. Nucor already raised HRC to $1,040 per ton, Steel Dynamics guided Q1 earnings nearly double year over year on a 35 percent backlog increase, and Cleveland-Cliffs sits in the same position. Any company with end-to-end US steel or aluminum supply chains gains pricing power.

What is drawback and who qualifies?

Drawback lets importers claim a tariff refund if the finished goods are re-exported to qualifying trade partners, which currently include the UK, EU, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, and Canada. It requires documentation at entry and at export, which favors large multinationals with trade compliance infrastructure.

Is this tariff structure likely to survive legal challenges?

The Express Fasteners lawsuit in Illinois is challenging CBP's assessment method on finished imports, specifically the 15 percent content threshold enforcement. A win would force clearer guidance but probably not overturn the tariff itself. Section 232 has survived prior legal challenges under national security justification.
