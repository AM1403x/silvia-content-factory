The Great Decoupling: Why Stocks Are Hitting Highs While 10 Year Yields Sit at 4.34%

The S&P 500 is at 6,611.83, a hair off an all time high, while the 10 year Treasury is yielding 4.339% and the 30 year is at 4.893%. Small caps, the group that's supposed to hate high rates, just printed a record high and are beating the Nasdaq-100 by 8 percentage points year to date.

If you learned finance in the last 40 years, none of that should be happening together. The old playbook says high rates crush stocks, especially small caps. That rule is broken, and the reason it's broken matters for every dollar you allocate from here.

The numbers that don't fit the old model

Let's get the full snapshot on the table. The S&P 500 is at 6,611.83. The Russell 2000 just hit a record high. The 2 year Treasury is at 3.852%, the 10 year at 4.339%, the 30 year at 4.893%. The Fed funds rate is at 3.50 to 3.75%, after the Fed cut four times in late 2025 to bring it down from 4.75%. The equity risk premium, the spread between the S&P earnings yield and the 10 year Treasury yield, is at a multi year low.

Under the traditional model, an equity risk premium this thin would mean stocks are dangerously overvalued relative to bonds. Investors should be selling stocks to buy Treasuries at 4.34%. That's not what's happening. Instead, money is rotating out of mega cap tech and into small caps, value, and cyclicals. The market is saying earnings are going to grow into the valuation, not the other way around.

What the small cap rally is actually telling you

Small caps are the most rate sensitive equities in the market. They carry more floating rate debt, refinance more often, and depend more on credit availability. When rates were climbing in 2022 and 2023, the Russell 2000 lagged the S&P by a wide margin for exactly that reason.

Now rates are still high, but small caps are leading. The explanation is simple. Investors are looking past the current rate environment to the next 12 months, and they're pricing in a soft landing where earnings recover before the Fed has to cut again. The Russell 2000's 8 point outperformance over the Nasdaq-100 year to date is not a defensive rotation. It's a bet that the cycle is turning up, not down.

Value and cyclical sectors confirm the story. Energy, industrials, materials, and financials are all leading growth and tech. That's a late cycle to early cycle rotation, and it only makes sense if you believe the economy is accelerating, not slowing.

The correlation break, in one table

Stock and bond yields used to be negatively correlated. When yields spiked, stocks sold off. When yields fell, stocks rallied. That rule held for most of the post 2008 era. It's been breaking for about two years now, and this year it's fully decoupled.

Either the market is right about the soft landing and this is the beginning of a real earnings cycle, or the market is wrong and this is a repricing waiting to happen.

What it means for your allocation

If you've been underweight small caps and value because you believed the "rates up, stocks down" rule, you've been wrong for 18 months and the cost is showing up in your return. The decoupling is now long enough and broad enough that it's not a fluke. It's a regime.

But here's the take. Regimes end. The fact that this rally is broadening is healthy, but the equity risk premium is at multi year lows, which means you're not being paid much to take equity risk over the 10 year Treasury. If the soft landing doesn't land, the adjustment isn't going to be pretty, and small caps with their thinner balance sheets will hurt the most.

The practical move is to own the rotation but hedge the thesis. If you're at a 60/40, think about taking your equity 60 and splitting it 40 large cap, 15 small/mid cap, and 5 international value. Keep your 40 bonds in short duration, not long duration, so you're not exposed if yields grind higher. And keep some dry powder. Equity risk premiums this thin tend to mean revert, not extend forever.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are small caps beating tech if rates are still high?

Because the market is pricing a soft landing where earnings recover. Small caps have thinner margins and higher fixed costs, so if the economy accelerates even modestly, their earnings grow faster than mega cap tech, which is already at peak margins.

What's the equity risk premium and why does it matter?

It's the spread between the S&P 500 earnings yield and the 10 year Treasury yield. When it's high, stocks are cheap relative to bonds. When it's low, like now, stocks are expensive relative to bonds, and the margin for error on earnings is thin.

Should I sell my long duration bonds?

If you bought them for the 2022 playbook of "crisis means Fed cuts means TLT rallies," you should know that playbook doesn't work in an inflationary environment. Short duration Treasuries give you most of the yield without the duration risk.

Is this really a soft landing or is the market wrong?

Honest answer, nobody knows yet. But the fact that small caps, value, and cyclicals are all leading is the market's vote for a soft landing. If that trade unwinds, you'll see it in credit spreads first, then in small cap underperformance. Watch those two indicators.

Does the decoupling mean I should ignore bond yields going forward?

No. It means the relationship is more complicated than the simple rule. High yields can coexist with a stock rally if earnings growth is strong enough. But if earnings growth stalls and yields stay high, the decoupling reverses fast.
