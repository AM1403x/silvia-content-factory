Consumer Credit Grew Just 2.2% in February: What a Slow Borrower Tells You About Main Street

Total U.S. consumer credit grew at a 2.2 percent seasonally adjusted annual rate in February, according to the Federal Reserve's G.19 report released Tuesday, with revolving credit crawling at just 0.6 percent annualized. That's the slowest pace of card borrowing since the 2020 lockdown months.

When you see card balances stall like that while auto and student loans keep growing at 2.8 percent, it's not a healthy consumer. It's either a tapped-out one or a scared one, and both paths lead to the same Q2 earnings problem.

What the G.19 Print Actually Says

The headline 2.2 percent annualized growth sounds fine on a flat read. It isn't. Revolving credit, which is almost entirely credit cards, is the real-time read on how Americans feel about spending they can't pay for out of the checking account. A 0.6 percent annualized rate means the aggregate card balance barely budged in February, and with the average card APR sitting around 21 to 22 percent, "barely budged" means households are finally feeling the interest cost.

Nonrevolving credit, which captures auto loans and student debt, grew at 2.8 percent. That's still positive but it's mostly inertia. Auto loans get originated when people buy cars, and student loan balances shift based on repayment schedules. Neither is a clean read on discretionary behavior. Revolving is the one that tells you whether the couch at Wayfair or the sneakers at Foot Locker are getting swiped or left on the shelf.

Put the math together. Cards are flat, APRs are at generational highs, and the nonrevolving bucket is slowing too. Households aren't deleveraging by choice. They're hitting the ceiling and parking it there.

The Small Business Tell Nobody Talked About

The G.19 didn't come out in isolation Tuesday. The NFIB Small Business Optimism Index for February printed at 98.8, down from 99.3 in January. That's the second monthly decline in a row and it's below the 51-year average of 98. Small business owners aren't optimists by nature, but when they stop hiring and stop raising prices, the thing they're feeling is weaker foot traffic.

Plus, the ISM Services Employment index came in at 45.2, the first contraction reading in four months. Services is 78 percent of the U.S. economy, and the employment sub-index is one of the cleaner leading indicators for the nonfarm payrolls number. When services start shedding workers and consumers stop swiping, you're not looking at a soft patch. You're looking at the front edge of a demand rollover.

Who Gets Hurt First and the Names on the Watch List

Discretionary retailers with thin margins and high ticket prices are where the pain lands first. If you're selling a $900 couch or a $65 sweater on impulse, and the impulse just got taxed at 22 percent APR, that sale doesn't happen. The companies most exposed are the ones that built their growth story on "trade-up" spending from middle-income households over the last three years.

You can see it in the tape already. Consumer discretionary is one of the worst sector performers year to date, and that's before we've seen a single Q1 earnings print. The stocks are telegraphing the miss. The credit data is confirming the mechanism.

Why This Isn't Just a February Fluke

One slow month doesn't make a trend. Three do. Revolving credit growth has decelerated in each of the last three G.19 reports, and the average is now running well below the 2023-2024 range of 5 to 8 percent annualized. You don't get that pattern from weather or seasonal noise. You get it from a household balance sheet that ran out of room.

The bulls will tell you card growth is slow because savings rates are improving. They're not. The personal savings rate is still stuck near 4 percent, well below the pre-pandemic norm of 7 to 8 percent. If households were rebuilding savings, you'd see it in the BEA data. Instead you see flat savings, stalled card growth, and rising delinquencies on auto loans. That's a squeeze, not a rebuild.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Fed's G.19 report affect stock prices?

Not directly, but it feeds the demand picture. When revolving credit growth stalls, analysts cut discretionary retailer estimates over the following 30 to 60 days. The stocks usually move on the estimate cuts, not on the G.19 release itself.

Is 0.6 percent revolving credit growth recessionary?

It's recession-adjacent. The last three times revolving credit dropped below 1 percent annualized without a clear crisis cause (2008, 2020, 2024 briefly), consumer discretionary stocks underperformed the S&P 500 over the next two quarters. It's not a guarantee, but it's a pattern with a track record.

Which retail stocks are most insulated from a consumer slowdown?

Off-price names like TJX and Ross tend to pick up share when shoppers trade down. Dollar stores benefit when the squeeze hits the low end. Walmart gets the grocery safety bid. Costco is the membership moat. None are immune, but they're the ones that historically gain relative strength when credit tightens.

What would change your mind on the bearish consumer call?

A March G.19 print that reaccelerates revolving credit back above 2 percent, combined with an NFIB optimism reading that stabilizes above 100. Both would suggest February was a weather-related blip rather than a structural turn. Right now the data is leaning the other way.

When does Q1 retail earnings season start?

The big-box and specialty retailers report mid-May through early June because most of them operate on a fiscal calendar that lags the standard quarter. Target and Walmart typically report in the third week of May, which gives you five weeks to position ahead of the print.
