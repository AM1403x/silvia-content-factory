Delta Wednesday, CPI Thursday: Your Earnings Season Kickoff Playbook

Delta Air Lines (DAL) reports before the open Wednesday with consensus calling for 5.4% revenue growth and 34.3% EPS growth, Constellation Brands (STZ) reports after the close with options markets pricing a 5.60% move versus a typical 2.89%, and March CPI drops Thursday with consensus at 3.1% year-over-year. You've got three event days in four trading sessions.

If you're going to reposition ahead of Q1 earnings, you do it now, not after the bank tape next Tuesday. Here's the sector-by-sector positioning guide for the next seven sessions.

Delta Is the First Read on the Consumer

Delta's Wednesday print matters because airlines are the cleanest real-time proxy for discretionary spending, business travel demand, and fuel cost pass-through. Consensus has revenue at +5.4% year-over-year and EPS growth at +34.3%. That's a high bar, and it's already reflected in the stock heading into the print.

The number you actually care about is the Q2 fuel guide. Delta already flagged a $2 billion Iran fuel hit for Q2, with all-in fuel costs forecast at $4.30 per gallon. If they confirm that number, the stock sells off regardless of the Q1 beat. If they walk it back, the whole travel group catches a bid. Bookings commentary matters more than the headline.

Constellation Brands Is the Consumer Staples Tell

STZ reports Q4 after the close Wednesday with consensus at $1.71 EPS (up 35% year-over-year) on $1.87B in revenue (down 13% year-over-year). That combination, earnings up, revenue down, is what a late-cycle consumer staples company looks like when it's cutting costs faster than it's losing customers.

The tell is the options market. Implied move is 5.60% versus the 2.89% typical move for STZ earnings. Traders are pricing in a wider range, which means something is being repositioned. Either the shorts got aggressive into the print, or somebody knows the guide is going to move the stock. Watch the post-earnings call for the 2027 volume outlook on beer, which is the only thing keeping the top line alive.

CPI Is the Session That Matters Most

Thursday's March CPI print is the biggest macro event of the week. Consensus is 3.1% year-over-year headline, up from 2.4% prior. That is a massive acceleration, and it's mostly because of base effects and oil. The market already knows the headline is going up.

The real watch is the core number and the services sub-indexes. The ISM Services Prices component hit 70.7% last week, a 42-month high, which tells you services inflation is not cooling the way the Fed needs it to. If core CPI comes in at 3.2% or above, expect a sharp repricing of Fed expectations and a move higher in the 10-year yield. If it prints 2.9% or below, the doves get ammunition.

Currently, the 10-year sits at 4.35% and fed funds futures have a 94.8% probability of an April hold. A hot CPI doesn't change the April meeting. It changes the June meeting. That's where your positioning matters most.

How to Position Into the Week

If you're underweight energy and the Iran headlines stay hot, the window to add is closing. If you're long airlines into Delta and the stock is already up into the print, trim and reload post-announcement. If you're long banks into next Tuesday's JPM print, wait for CPI first. The print order matters.

The single cleanest trade is to avoid the surprise. You don't need to guess the direction on Delta. You need to have a plan for both outcomes and size positions so neither blows up the book. Wednesday's two earnings reports are volatile enough that being small is the right answer if you're unsure.

CPI is the one you can't avoid. If you own duration, a hot print hurts. If you own inflation beneficiaries like energy and commodities, a hot print helps. Make sure the portfolio is balanced before Thursday morning, not after.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the biggest risk to Delta's Q1 print?

The Q2 fuel guide. Delta flagged $2 billion in Iran-related fuel pressure and $4.30 per gallon all-in costs. If they reaffirm that guide, the Q1 beat gets wiped out by the Q2 concern. The stock trades on forward guidance, not backward results.

Why is Constellation's options implied move so high?

At 5.60% implied vs 2.89% typical, the market is pricing in a meaningful surprise. It's usually driven by the Q4 beer volume guide and the 2027 outlook, which STZ has been struggling to defend. Wide implied moves are an indicator that positioning is stretched.

How hot can CPI get before the Fed responds?

Core CPI above 3.3% is the level that starts changing the June FOMC conversation. Below that, the committee can hold the "watchful waiting" line. Above that, you get hawkish speaker commentary and a repricing of the dot plot at the April 29 meeting.

Is it too late to rotate before CPI?

No, but the window is closing. You've got until Wednesday's close to reposition into Thursday's print. After that, you're stuck with whatever you own. The easiest hedge is to reduce duration and add short-dated inflation beneficiaries.

What's the tell on bank earnings next Tuesday?

Net interest margin and credit loss provisions are the two numbers you care about. If NIM is flat to up and provisions are down, banks rally. If NIM is down and provisions are up, the whole group sells off. The commercial real estate commentary is the secondary watch item.
