Big Bank Earnings Cheat Sheet: JPM Needs $42.8B to Hold the Line

JPMorgan reports Tuesday morning with consensus at $4.32 EPS on $42.8 billion in revenue, and the stock is going in with the KBE bank ETF down 3.2% year to date against an S&P 500 that's still up 1.8%.

That gap tells you everything about the setup. The financials trade has broken down while the broad market has held. Next week's prints from JPM, GS, MS, BAC, WFC, and C are the report card on whether the bank trade gets rebuilt or gets written off for the year.

The Reporting Calendar

Six of the eight most important bank names land inside a 36-hour window. Tuesday April 14 before the open delivers JPM, WFC, C, and GS back to back. Wednesday April 15 before the open rounds it out with MS, BAC, and BLK. If you're holding bank exposure, your entire week is decided between 6 AM and 9 AM ET on those two days.

The consensus bar is not high. That matters because stocks are going in beaten up, and beaten-up stocks plus low bars equal positive skew into the print.

The stocks are cheap into the print because the curve keeps flattening. Ten-year at 4.34% and two-year at 4.18% puts the 2s10s at just 16 basis points, which isn't technically inverted but isn't giving banks any help on the net interest margin story. Recession chatter is back, credit card delinquencies are climbing, and the market is pricing banks like the cycle already turned.

Net Interest Income Is Line One

JPM's Q4 guidance for 2026 full-year NII was $87 billion. Divide that evenly and the Q1 run rate should be about $21.75 billion. If the actual print lands at $21.5 billion or better, Jamie Dimon will probably reiterate the $87 billion number and the stock holds its ground. Below $21.25 billion and you're looking at a guidance cut, which means the $4.32 consensus EPS becomes a ceiling for the rest of the year.

JPM is the bellwether. If JPM holds the line, the rest of the group gets permission to disappoint on other metrics. If JPM cuts, WFC and C get hammered because they're more NII-dependent and have fewer fee-revenue offsets.

For WFC, the $20.4 billion consensus embeds a modest NII decline from Q4. A print that shows stabilization in deposit costs is the single most bullish thing WFC management can deliver. C is trickier because so much of the business is trading and treasury services. For Citi, anchor on the expense line instead, since that's where Jane Fraser's turnaround story lives or dies.

Trading Revenue Gets a Tailwind

The VIX averaged 18 in Q1 versus 14 in Q4. That's a 29% jump in realized volatility, and higher volatility almost always translates into stronger fixed income trading at the investment banks. GS and MS are the two names that matter here. JPM's trading arm is a distant third tier of importance to the overall print.

Goldman's $9.12 EPS consensus is already pricing in a reasonable trading quarter. The surprise, if there is one, will come from the equities desk on prime brokerage and derivatives flow. That's not in the published model for most sell-side analysts. If GS puts up a $10 number, the stock probably gaps 4% higher.

Morgan Stanley's story is different. MS is a wealth management franchise that also has a bank attached, so the trading upside at MS is smaller. What matters more for Morgan is the asset under management print and the advisory pipeline commentary. If AUM is flat or down, the whole MS multiple re-rating thesis breaks.

Loan Loss Provisions Are the Soft Spot

Consumer credit grew just 2.2% year over year in February, a deceleration from higher levels during the 2023 to 2024 recovery. Credit card delinquencies ticked up to 3.4% versus 2.9% a year ago. When delinquencies rise and credit growth slows at the same time, banks have to take bigger provisions.

The question is which bank bit the bullet in Q4 and which one is still pretending. JPM already added $2.3 billion to reserves in Q4, which probably means Dimon shows a smaller build this quarter. C was late to build reserves last year, so Citi is more likely to have a messy provision line. WFC is the wild card because it runs low reserves relative to peers. If Q1 provisions jump, the stock gaps 5% lower in the premarket.

The Pair Trade

Here's the setup. Stocks going in beaten up. NII guidance under pressure. Trading revenue tailwind from Q1 volatility. Provisions trending worse. The banks are not all exposed to those forces equally.

Long quality, short pure-credit. JPM and GS are the quality side because they have fee revenue diversification and the strongest balance sheets. C and WFC are the pure-credit side because they're most exposed to deposit costs, provision builds, and the flattening curve. If you run the long JPM plus long GS against short C plus short WFC as a 50-50 basket on each side, you're expressing the view that the industry dispersion will widen into the prints.

That trade worked in 2023 and 2024. It probably works again this cycle because the forces driving dispersion haven't changed. Quality banks earn through the cycle. Pure credit banks earn when credit is easy and suffer when credit tightens. We're in the suffering phase.

My take. I'd go into the print leaning long JPM and long GS, with matched hedges on C and WFC. Don't own BAC going in because BAC has the worst deposit franchise of the big four and the NII story is the weakest. BLK is a separate trade entirely because it's an asset manager not a bank, and the only thing that moves BLK is net flows and fee rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the most important number to watch when JPM reports?

Net interest income run rate. JPM guided to $87 billion for full year 2026. The Q1 print should be roughly $21.5 billion to $22 billion to keep that guide intact. If Dimon holds the line on NII, the stock holds. If he cuts the guide, expect a 3% to 5% gap down and the rest of the sector follows.

Why are bank stocks down while the S&P is up?

Three reasons. The yield curve is flat, which compresses net interest margins. Credit card delinquencies are rising, which signals bigger provisions ahead. And recession fears have shifted rate cut expectations, which hurts banks on both the loan growth and deposit cost side. The S&P doesn't care about those three things to the same degree.

Is GS a better buy than MS into the prints?

Yes if you're betting on trading revenue. GS has roughly double the fixed income trading exposure of MS and benefits more from the Q1 volatility spike. MS is a wealth management story, and the AUM number is what matters for that stock. Different bets, different risks, same reporting week.

What happens if a bank misses badly?

A 5% to 8% single-day drop on the name, and a 2% to 3% drag on the broader KBE ETF. The last time JPM missed meaningfully was in 2022, and the stock gave back two months of gains in one session. That's the downside risk to sizing up into the print.

Should I buy the KBE ETF instead of picking names?

Only if you have no view on dispersion. KBE owns the whole group equal-weighted, which means you're paying for the C and WFC risk without getting enough JPM and GS upside. If you have a directional view that all banks will rally, KBE is fine. If you have any sector-specific take, individual names give you better risk-adjusted exposure.
