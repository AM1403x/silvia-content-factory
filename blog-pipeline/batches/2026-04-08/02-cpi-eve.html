CPI Eve: Three Scenarios, One Print, and Why the MOVE Index at 95 Tells You Bonds Are Nervous

Consensus for tomorrow's 8:30 AM ET CPI release is headline 2.6% year over year and core 2.9% year over year, with both headline and core monthly changes pegged at 0.3%.

That's the number. The setup is what matters. The MOVE index, which measures implied volatility in Treasuries, sits at 95 versus a long-run average near 80. Bond traders are pricing in more risk around tomorrow's print than they have for any single data release since the last Fed meeting.

What's Priced

Fed funds futures are giving you 18% odds of a rate cut at the April 29 FOMC, 38% by June, and 58% by July. That July number is the one to watch. It's the market saying "one cut by summer, probably." Any reading that changes the June probability materially will move the entire curve.

The 10-year Treasury yield is at 4.34%. The 2-year sits at 4.18%. That's a normalized curve, barely steepened, with both ends hovering at levels that imply traders think we're near the peak but aren't pricing the start of a cutting cycle. One soft CPI print flips that. One hot print validates the patient camp and breaks the curve wider.

The Three Scenarios

There are really only three ways this prints, and each one has a clean trade associated with it.

The hot print is the painful one. At 0.4% core monthly, you're running at almost 5% annualized, which is nowhere near the 2% target. The June cut dies. Probably the July cut dies too. Williams, who said Tuesday the Fed is in "watchful waiting" mode, would be vindicated. The 10-year breaks 4.50% and the S&P absorbs a 1 to 1.5% down day. Small caps get hit harder because they carry more floating-rate debt.

The in-line print is the boring one, and boring is good for risk assets. Rates don't move. The cut probability stays near 38%. Equities drift. You'd see some rotation inside the tape but the index close is flat to up 0.3%. Most desks are positioned for this.

The cool print is the big one. At 0.2% core monthly, you're annualizing at 2.4% and suddenly the disinflation narrative gets fresh legs. Fed funds futures push the June cut above 60%. The 10-year breaks 4.20%. Small caps rip because the Russell 2000 is the most rate-sensitive part of the equity market, and they haven't participated in the 2026 rally.

What the Inflation Hawks Will Point To

The ISM Services Prices index printed 70.7% in the latest release. That's a number you shouldn't ignore. Services inflation has been the sticky part of the CPI story for two years now, and 70.7% is a reading you don't see outside a genuinely tight pricing environment. Hawks will tell you this is the reason core stays at 0.3% or prints 0.4%, because services shelter and medical care aren't going to cooperate.

The doves will point to goods deflation, the still-cooling rent line, and the fact that used cars have rolled over again. Both sides have real data. That's why the MOVE is at 95 and not 80.

How To Position Into the Print

You don't try to pick the number. Desks that build a business trading CPI have models and alternative data feeds you can't replicate from your kitchen table. What you can do is stress-test your book against each of the three outcomes.

If you're long small caps, a hot print is your worst case. If you're long duration Treasuries, a hot print is a drawdown. If you're long quality mega-caps with steady earnings, the tape can absorb any of the three scenarios without killing you. The cool print rewards the risk-on trade across the board but it's the lowest-probability scenario right now. Most sellside desks are pricing this as roughly 60% in-line, 25% hot, 15% cool.

The take: the MOVE at 95 tells you the bond market isn't confident. That's a warning, not a signal. Respect the volatility, size accordingly, and don't bet the farm on the tail. The smart money waits for the print and then reacts, because the real opportunity isn't in the first 30 seconds after 8:30 AM. It's in the drift over the next two sessions as the curve digests what the print actually means for the June meeting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the MOVE index at 95 matter?

The MOVE index measures implied volatility in Treasury options, the bond market's version of the VIX. At 95 versus an 80 average, it's telling you bond traders are paying up to hedge against a big move in either direction. That's an environment where you don't want to be over-positioned going into a data release.

What's the difference between headline and core CPI?

Headline CPI includes everything. Core CPI strips out food and energy because those two categories are volatile and don't reflect underlying inflation trends. The Fed focuses on core because it tells you where inflation is actually running before weather and OPEC decisions get in the way.

What does "watchful waiting" mean from Williams?

Williams, the New York Fed president, said Tuesday the Fed is in a patient, data-dependent mode. Translation: they aren't going to cut until they see multiple months of clean data confirming the disinflation. One soft print isn't enough. They want the trend.

Why is Russell 2000 the asymmetric trade on a cool print?

Small caps in the Russell 2000 carry much more floating-rate debt than S&P large caps. When rates fall, their interest expense drops immediately. They also tend to be more domestically focused and rate-sensitive, so any shift in Fed expectations hits them harder on the upside and the downside.

How much of tomorrow's move is already in the tape?

Some, but not most. Equities drifted sideways into the print. Bonds actually sold off modestly over the last two days, which suggests positioning tilted slightly toward a hot outcome. That's why an in-line print could still get a small relief rally in rates even without any surprise.
