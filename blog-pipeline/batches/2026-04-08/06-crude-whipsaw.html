Crude Whipsaw: WTI Ran $112 to $98 to $104 in Three Sessions

WTI crude traded in a $14 band this week, hitting $98.20 intraday Wednesday morning on ceasefire headlines before closing back at $104.10, a day after settling at $106.90 and three trading days after a $112.40 close on Friday.

Five-day realized volatility is running at 68% annualized against a 30-day average of 28%. The OVX, which is effectively the VIX for oil, sits at 52 versus a normal level around 30. That's not a direction trade anymore. The volatility is the trade.

What Happened

Friday April 3 closed at $112.40 on the back of intelligence reports that Iran was preparing to restrict Hormuz transit. Monday April 6 settled at $109.80 as the market digested what Strait of Hormuz disruption actually means for seaborne crude flows. We walked through that scenario in Monday's Hormuz Hedging piece.

Tuesday April 7 closed at $106.90 as options desks started rolling long positions further out the curve. That was the day we covered the Energy Ceasefire Cliff setup, pointing out that XOM, CVX, and OXY were trading like a war premium was locked in.

Wednesday April 8 broke the move. A Reuters report hit the tape before 7 AM ET saying Iran and the US were running back-channel talks via Oman on Hormuz access. WTI gapped from $106 to $101 in the first thirty minutes, sold to an intraday low of $98.20 by 10 AM, then reversed and closed at $104.10. An $8 round trip on a $104 underlying is 7.7% of the commodity in a single session.

The Volatility Numbers

Realized volatility is the metric that matters here, not closing price. When five-day realized vol runs at 68% annualized against a 30-day average of 28%, you have a 2.4x multiple of normal activity. That divergence rarely lasts. Either long-dated vol catches up and fireworks continue, or short-term vol compresses back toward the mean.

The USO oil ETF is trading at three times its normal daily volume. That's retail money and systematic trend-following CTAs both piling in. Retail chases the headline move. CTAs chase the realized vol breakout. When both hit the tape together, you get gaps and reversals that don't respect technical levels.

The Brent-WTI spread at $4.20 versus a normal $4.00 is the most boring number in the table, and that's the tell. If Brent were spiking relative to WTI, the market would be pricing physical disruption to seaborne crude. It isn't. The market is pricing headline risk, not physical risk.

The Energy Stock Reaction

Energy stocks got crushed Wednesday on the ceasefire headline even though crude closed the session well above its lows. The big names all finished red. XOM was down 2.8%, CVX was down 3.1%, OXY dropped 4.6%, and EOG gave back 3.9%. This is the exact setup we flagged Tuesday, where the large caps were priced for the war premium to stick.

That's because the upstream business has direct price sensitivity, while XOM and CVX have downstream refining that actually benefits when crude falls. When you see this pattern, it tells you the market is repricing war premium out of the oil complex, not rotating between sub-sectors.

Refiners are the hidden winner nobody talked about Wednesday. VLO and MPC both finished green while the upstream names were red. When crude cracks, input costs fall faster than gasoline prices do, which widens the crack spread and expands refiner margins. That's a textbook relationship and it showed up on the tape in real time.

The Play

Here's the trade. Stop trying to pick direction. The move from $112 to $98 to $104 in three sessions tells you that directional bets get whipsawed. Volatility is running at 68% against a baseline of 28%, which means options premiums are rich but the realized delivery is even richer.

Long straddle on USO with April monthly options costs approximately 8% of underlying at current implied vol levels. Realized vol for the past five days has been 68%. The math says you need a move of about 14% in either direction over the next two weeks for the straddle to work, and the commodity just delivered an 8% range in a single session. If realized vol stays anywhere close to current levels, the straddle prints.

The risk is obvious. If Friday delivers a confirmed ceasefire and the Strait of Hormuz issue dies, WTI could settle into a $95 to $100 range and volatility collapses fast. Your straddle loses time value and theta eats you alive. The other risk is the opposite. If Friday delivers a breakdown in negotiations, WTI gaps through $115 at the Sunday open and the call side pays you instantly.

If you don't want to own options, the next best play is long VLO or MPC as a crack spread proxy. Refiners benefit in either scenario. If crude stays elevated, crack spreads stay wide because gasoline demand is inelastic in the short run. If crude crashes on a ceasefire, crack spreads widen because gasoline lags crude on the way down. Either way, refiner margins expand.

My take. I'd rather own the straddle than the refiners right now because the volatility premium is too cheap relative to the realized moves we just watched. But if you're not comfortable with options and you want exposure, VLO and MPC are the cleanest single-name expressions of the same trade.

What To Watch Friday

Three things flip the setup. First, confirmation or denial on the Oman-mediated talks. A State Department briefing or an Iranian foreign ministry statement does it. Second, the weekly EIA inventory print next Wednesday. Third, OPEC plus commentary. If Saudi Arabia signals a production response, the whole curve moves.

Friday is the inflection. Going into a weekend with this kind of realized vol, the market will demand a resolution or a fresh escalation. Sitting flat isn't a stable state. Options dealers are already positioning for the gap and retail is positioned the wrong way as usual.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why did WTI drop so fast Wednesday morning?

A Reuters report before 7 AM ET said Iran and the US were holding back-channel talks via Oman on Strait of Hormuz access. The market had been pricing in a continued escalation scenario, and the report flipped the narrative toward possible de-escalation. That's what caused the $8 intraday move from the open to the 10 AM low.

Is the long straddle actually a good trade?

It is if realized vol stays anywhere near 68% annualized. The April monthly straddle on USO costs around 8% of spot, and breakeven is roughly a 14% move over two weeks. The commodity delivered nearly that entire range in one session Wednesday. Implied vol is still underpricing what's been happening, so the math favors the buyer of optionality.

Why are refiners winning in both scenarios?

Refiners make money on the crack spread, which is the difference between crude input cost and gasoline output price. When crude falls fast, gasoline prices take longer to reprice at the pump, so the spread widens. When crude rises fast, gasoline also rises but lags, so the spread compresses briefly then stabilizes. Over any two-week window with high volatility, crack spreads average wider than in calm markets. That's why VLO and MPC finished green Wednesday while XOM and CVX finished red.

What happens if the Hormuz talks fall apart Friday?

WTI gaps back to $115 or higher at the Sunday open. Energy stocks retrace all of Wednesday's losses and then some. The straddle call side pays instantly. The risk is that the move reverses just as fast on Monday as fresh headlines hit. You're in a high-volatility regime where every new data point moves the market 5% to 8%.

Why does Brent-WTI spread matter here?

If the market were pricing actual physical disruption to seaborne crude, Brent would blow out versus WTI. Brent is the seaborne benchmark that would get hit first by any Hormuz closure. The spread at $4.20 versus a normal $4.00 is essentially unchanged, which tells you the market is pricing headline risk on WTI, not physical disruption. That's why this feels like a volatility trade more than a directional trade.
