Steel Stocks One Week Into Section 232: NUE Up 7.4%, CLF Up 11.3%

Nucor closed at $189 on Wednesday, up 7.4% on the week, after peaking at $192 on Tuesday in the fastest two-session rally the stock has posted in 2026.

Section 232 tariffs took effect on April 6, slapping 50% on steel imports and 25% on aluminum. One week in, the domestic steel basket has ripped and the options market is paying up for more upside. The question every CFO should be asking: is this the new floor or a sentiment spike that fades by May?

What Happened This Week

The tariff went live Monday, April 6. NUE opened the week at $176, gapped to $188 at the open, and peaked Tuesday at $192. By Wednesday's close it settled at $189, still up 7.4% for the week. That's a $13 per share move on no earnings, no guidance, no sell-side upgrade. Pure policy tailwind.

The other integrated and minimill names followed the same pattern. US Steel (X) moved from $42 to $46, a 9.5% weekly gain. Cleveland-Cliffs (CLF) ripped from $14.20 to $15.80, up 11.3%. Steel Dynamics (STLD) added 6.8%, climbing from $148 to $158.

This extends the narrative we laid out in Monday's Nucor deep dive on the ninth straight price hike. Nucor was already pushing domestic hot-rolled coil higher before the tariff landed. The tariff gave them cover to keep pushing.

The real tell isn't the stock prices. It's the options flow. Volume in NUE call options ran at three times the average daily rate on Monday and Tuesday. The most active contract was the front-month $200 strike, which means traders are paying up for the chance NUE adds another 6% in the next four weeks.

Front-month calls are the cheapest way to press a news-driven trade. When you see them dominate the flow, it means institutional desks aren't worried about a reversal, they're worried about missing the next leg. That's bullish positioning, but it's also fragile. If NUE fades back to $180, those $200 calls become worthless in a hurry.

HRC Prices and the Import Math

Hot-rolled coil (HRC) spot prices tell the real story. Pre-tariff, HRC was at $815 per ton. By Wednesday, HRC was quoted at $920 per ton. That's a $105 move in four trading days, or about 12.9% higher.

That price move matters because it flows directly to the bottom line at every integrated steel producer. CLF has the highest operating sensitivity to HRC because its cost base is mostly fixed (iron ore, coke, labor). A $100 move in HRC is close to a $100 move in gross margin per ton on CLF's 17 million annual tons of shipment capacity. Do that math and CLF's EBITDA profile changes fast.

NUE has less direct exposure to HRC because it runs minimills that flex on scrap prices, but minimills still capture most of the benefit when spot prices hold. STLD sits in a similar spot, with about 13 million tons of flat-rolled capacity.

The cleanest reason to believe this rally has legs is the import substitution math. Imported steel was 24% of US consumption in 2025. With 50% tariffs on the main foreign suppliers, that share should compress to 12% to 15% by year-end.

US mill capacity utilization is running at 76%, which means there's meaningful headroom to absorb the displaced imports. If utilization climbs to 85%, that's an additional 12 million tons of domestic production picked up by the same capital base. Fixed cost absorption improves, margins expand, and earnings ratchet up for three to four quarters even without another HRC price move.

The Counterargument

Every trade has a counter, and this one has two that you can't ignore. First, end-user pain. The auto industry consumes about 14% of US steel. Analysts on the autos desk are already modeling $50 to $90 cost increases per vehicle, which ripples into margin pressure at Ford, GM, and Stellantis. That cost gets passed through to consumers, which means higher vehicle prices at a time when auto affordability is already at multi-decade lows.

Second, construction pass-through. Commercial real estate developers are absorbing an estimated 8% steel cost increase on projects in the bid stage. That will either get passed into higher rents, which the CRE market cannot absorb, or it will kill marginal projects and slow construction orders. Either way, steel demand from construction softens after the initial restocking pulse.

The bull case assumes the import substitution benefit outweighs the demand destruction from higher prices. That's a net-net judgment call, and we won't know for sure until Q2 earnings in late July.

The Setup

Three ways to play it. NUE is the highest quality operator, with the strongest balance sheet and the best capital allocation track record. It's also the most priced, trading at a premium multiple to the group. If you want the safest exposure, NUE is your name, but your upside is probably capped at $210 in a 60-day window.

CLF has the highest operating sensitivity to HRC. If spot stays above $900 through Q2, CLF's earnings estimate revisions are going to be huge. The risk is CLF also has the most debt in the group, and a reversal in HRC hits CLF harder than anyone. Higher beta, higher risk, higher reward.

US Steel is the M&A wildcard. The Nippon Steel deal is still in regulatory limbo, and any new development, positive or negative, moves X by 10% in a session. You're buying a policy-driven steel stock plus an M&A option. Not for the risk-averse.

STLD is the quiet middle path. Good balance sheet, good operating model, less glamorous than NUE, less dramatic than CLF. If you can't pick between the top three, STLD is the "I don't have to think about it" name.

My take. I'd rather own CLF from here to July earnings if HRC stays above $900. The operating sensitivity story is the cleanest. But I'd pair it with a small short on KMX or an auto OEM to hedge the end-user pain that's coming in Q3.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of the steel rally is already priced in?

Probably half to two-thirds. The stocks moved on the tariff announcement weeks ago and the effective date on April 6 added fresh momentum. The remaining upside depends on HRC staying above $900 per ton and capacity utilization climbing toward 85%. If both happen, NUE can hit $210 and CLF can hit $18.

What's the biggest risk to this trade?

Demand destruction. If auto OEMs push back hard on the cost pass-through, construction orders slow, and CRE bids drop off, the HRC price rally stalls by June. Once that happens, the operating sensitivity story reverses. The signal to watch is the weekly HRC spot print from CRU or Platts.

Why is CLF outperforming the group?

Two reasons. First, CLF has the highest operating sensitivity to HRC prices because its cost base is more fixed than NUE's. Second, CLF is the lowest-priced stock in the group on an absolute basis, so a small dollar move is a big percentage move. The 11.3% weekly gain is flattered by the low share price.

Is this a trade or a hold?

It's a trade until Q2 earnings in late July. That's when you find out whether the volume pickup from import substitution offsets the demand pressure from higher prices. If the Q2 prints come in strong, it converts to a hold into Q3. If they disappoint, you ring the register and walk away.

How does this compare to the 2018 tariff cycle?

The 2018 Section 232 tariffs were 25% on steel and 10% on aluminum, and the steel basket rallied roughly 15% over the next three months before fading. The current 50% tariff is twice as aggressive, and the starting position on capacity utilization (76% vs 79% in 2018) leaves more room for margin expansion. This cycle has more room to run if the policy holds, but the political risk of retaliation is higher.
